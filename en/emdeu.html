00 · Overview

EMDEU isn't a single app — it's four connected products: a patient mobile app, a video consultation module (telemedicine), a web admin panel for doctors built on the full ICD-10 registry across 18 specialties, and a psychological assessment module developed jointly with three medical universities.

A previous designer had worked on the project before me, but only on the patient app. By the time I came in, there was no shared system — every product lived by its own rules. I joined to bring it all into a single ecosystem: one visual language, one logic, one user experience — for the patient, the doctor, and the front-desk staff.

01 · Context

Before I joined.

The previous designer had worked only on the patient app. The visuals were overloaded — heavy type, flat illustrations, aggressive purple, small text. For a medical product where the audience runs from teenagers to retirees, that didn't work.

The bigger problem was that there was no system. Every screen lived by its own rules. Once the doctor's panel, telemedicine, and surveys were added to the scope, it was clear: we had to start from scratch.

02 · Redesign

What I changed.

Four directions that pulled the rest of the system with them.

01

Typography

Moved away from aggressive bold sans. Picked a calmer typeface with large numerals — because what matters most in a medical app is blood pressure, pulse, temperature. An elderly patient should be able to read them without glasses.

02

Illustrations

Replaced flat line icons with 3D illustrations of devices: blood pressure monitor, glucose meter, thermometer. The patient knows what's what at a glance — no labels needed.

03

Color

Dropped the aggressive purple. Moved to neutral medical tones with a single accent color. Color works for trust, not for attention.

04

Hierarchy

Cards instead of flat lists. Breathing room between blocks. Large touch targets for older users. The patient finds what they need in 2 seconds, not 10.

05 · Telemedicine

Video consultation with a doctor.

The video call is the most stressful moment for the patient. Camera lags, no clarity on when it starts, no idea what to do afterward — and the person drops back to an in-person clinic.

So the flow is split into four screens with clear roles. The waiting room shows a countdown and the doctor's name. The connecting step checks the camera. The call itself is interface-free — just the doctor and an end-call button. After the session — a recording and recommendations to revisit a week later.

06 · Doctor Admin

Web admin built on the ICD-10 registry.

The registry covers 18 specialties — from infectious and endocrine diseases to trauma and mental health. The doctor picks their specialty, sees their patients within that category, opens a card, and runs the examination in a format that matches their profile.

A rheumatologist's examination uses a joint diagram and the FRAX, T-score, and DAS28 scales. A cardiologist gets a different field set. The final conclusion is auto-compiled from the card data into a formal stamped document — the doctor never retypes the case history.

07 · Surveys

Psychological assessments for three medical universities.

Three medical universities adopted EMDEU into their curriculum. Their clinical psychologists developed the questionnaires for students — Seziný, MMPI, anxiety and depression scales — and these are now available to every app user.

A win-win partnership: the universities got a tool for their students, EMDEU got clinically validated content.

One UX decision worth calling out — the result screen. Not a clinical score like 24/100, but a contextual phrase based on the outcome: "looks like you're a bit tired." For mental health, a student needs to feel support, not a diagnosis — and that call was made together with the universities' clinical psychologists.

08 · V2 Explorations

Measuring pulse with the phone camera.

PPG pulse measurement using the smartphone camera and flash. The finger covers the lens, the flash shines through the tissue, the camera records fluctuations in reflected light — and the heart rate is computed from that signal.

The hard part wasn't the visual — it was managing the user's attention across 20 seconds of measurement. Progress runs along the edge of the screen, BPM updates in real time, and at the end — a large number and an "Add to tracker" button.

09 · Process

Sketches before Figma.

In healthcare, research always comes back to one constraint — access. Clinic NDAs and medical data regulations prevent direct patient interviews without a separate legal procedure. So I built understanding through a proxy source: the operations director, who works daily with both doctors and patients across the clinics. The trade-off was deliberate — pattern recognition across the entire operation, instead of conclusions drawn from 5–7 isolated interviews.

But every complex flow went through two stages before Figma. First a marker on a whiteboard — the architecture: who writes to whom, what links exist, what conditions apply. Then pencil on paper — wireframes of specific screens, hierarchy, buttons. It saves hours of iteration: you can see whether the logic works before you spend time on pixels.

10 · What I learned

The main lesson from this project —
work with structure.

199 screens across four connected products. One designer. Without structure that would have been impossible — every decision pulls dozens of other screens with it, and if you change a component in one place, you need to be sure it still works everywhere else.

EMDEU taught me to keep the system in my head even when there's no time to formalize it. This isn't about doing things "by the textbook" — it's that otherwise you simply drown in the volume of screens.

The other one — listen to the client, but think for the user. The operations director sees the project from inside the business. Doctors see it from inside the clinical process. Patients see it from inside their own pain and anxiety. A good designer stitches all three points of view into a single product.
